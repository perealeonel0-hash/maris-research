## A. BRAND IDENTITY FRAMEWORK (Chris Do / The Futur)

### Core Principle
> "A brand is a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or company. Branding is not what YOU say it is -- it's what THEY say it is." -- Marty Neumeier, quoted by Chris Do

### The Order Matters: Strategy Before Design
1. **Strategy** (invisible) -- positioning, audience, voice, differentiation
2. **Identity** (visible) -- logo, colors, typography, imagery
3. **Experience** (felt) -- every touchpoint from app to support to packaging

Jumping to logo before strategy = decorating a house with no foundation.

### Chris Do's Brand Strategy Process
1. **Discovery / Diagnostic** -- Why does this brand need to exist? What's happening in the market? Who is the customer? Not a "yes person" meeting -- honest advisory.
2. **Brand Attributes & Positioning** -- Facilitate word extraction from stakeholders. Boil down to ONE positioning phrase that captures the essence.
3. **Visual Exploration (Stylescape)** -- Collection of found images, patterns, marks, textures that REFLECT the brand attributes. Client approves direction before a single logo is drawn.
4. **Design Development** -- Sketching and drawing concepts that capture key attributes in the most interesting forms.
5. **Application & Mockups** -- Logo applied to contextually relevant surfaces (app screens, business cards, packaging).

Timeline: ~2 weeks discovery, 2-4 weeks design, 2 weeks finalization.

### The Brand Brief (what you define BEFORE design)
| Component | Question |
|---|---|
| **Mission** | Why do we exist beyond making money? |
| **Vision** | Where are we going in 5 years? |
| **Values** | What do we refuse to compromise on? |
| **Personality** | If the brand were a person, how would they talk? |
| **Voice** | Casual or formal? Direct or poetic? What words do we NEVER use? |
| **Audience** | Who specifically, and what do they care about? |
| **Positioning** | What makes us the ONLY option for this audience? |
| **Promise** | What do we guarantee every time? |

### Trust Triangle (Chris Do)
Three components that build brand trust:
- **Authenticity** -- being who you say you are
- **Empathy** -- genuinely caring about the user
- **Logic** -- making sense in your approach

### Content Ratio (9-1-1)
For every 9 pieces of value you give, 1 personal story, you earn 1 ask. Build trust through consistent delivery before asking for anything.

## B. NAMING FRAMEWORK

### Jonathan Bell's 7 Categories of Brand Names

| Category | Definition | Examples | Strength | Weakness |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Eponymous** | Named after a person (founder, inventor) | Disney, Tesla, Adidas, Burberry | Authority, legacy | Tied to one person |
| **Descriptive** | Says what it does | American Airlines, Home Depot, YouTube, PayPal | Instant clarity | Hard to trademark, generic feel |
| **Acronym** | Shortened descriptive | GE, BMW, IBM, KFC, HSBC | Clean, short | Meaningless without awareness |
| **Real Word (Suggestive)** | Existing word that hints at benefit | Uber, Slack, Amazon, Apple | Memorable, metaphorical | Hard to own SEO |
| **Composite** | Two words combined | Facebook, Ray-Ban, FedEx, Instagram | Descriptive + catchy | Can feel dated |
| **Invented** | Modified/created word | Kleenex, Pinterest, Spotify | Unique, trademarkable | Requires marketing investment |
| **Abstract** | No inherent meaning, pure phonetics | Rolex, Kodak, Xerox | Total blank slate | Requires massive brand building |

### The Naming Matrix (How Brands Are Built)
Two axes:
- **Vertical: Approach** -- Descriptive --> Suggestive --> Abstract
- **Horizontal: Construct** -- Real Word | Compound | Coined

This creates 9 zones:

```
                  Real Word      Compound        Coined
Abstract      |  Apple, Nike  |               | Kodak, Xerox  |
Suggestive    |  Amazon, Uber | BestBuy       | Febreze       |
Descriptive   |  Founders'    | HomeDepot     | Acronyms      |
```

### Bell's Rules for Great Names
1. **Names don't describe -- they STAND for a big idea** that translates into emotional appeal
2. **Reflect core values**: Nike = winning, GoPro = heroism, Apple = simplicity
3. **Check availability thoroughly** -- trademark + negative meanings in other languages
4. **Don't obsess over exact-match domains** -- search and social matter more now
5. **Think strategically** -- name should support vision beyond current product

### Naming Best Practices (2025-2026 research)
- **Length**: 5-12 letters optimal. Under 4 syllables.
- **Pronounceability**: If someone can't say it after hearing it once, it fails
- **Spellability**: If someone can't type it after hearing it once, it fails
- **Distinctiveness**: Must not sound like a competitor
- **Cross-language check**: Test in your top 5 markets minimum
- **Domain flexibility**: .com not required -- .app, .co, .io all viable now
- **App Store**: Search for conflicts in iOS App Store and Google Play specifically

### Context Rotation Technique
Take a word from one domain and apply it to another:
- **Apple** (fruit --> tech) = simplicity, naturalness
- **Amazon** (river --> retail) = vast, everything
- **Python** (snake --> programming) = power, flexibility
- **Slack** (leisure --> work tool) = ease, no pressure
- **Uber** (German "above" --> transport) = superior
This creates instant intrigue because the word has existing emotional weight but unexpected context.

### 2025-2026 Naming Trends
1. **Portmanteau names** -- blending two words (Pinterest = pin + interest, Instagram = instant + telegram)
2. **Single-word monikers** -- clean, one word (Stripe, Notion, Linear)
3. **Lowercase by default** -- brands launch in lowercase to feel approachable
4. **Nature/elemental words** -- calm, serene, flow (wellness space especially)
5. **Verb-like names** -- names that feel like actions (Zoom, Dash, Spark)

## C. VISUAL HIERARCHY FOR CONVERSION

### The Two Scanning Patterns

**Z-Pattern** (for minimal layouts, landing pages, app screens)
```
1. Top-left (logo/brand) -----> 2. Top-right (CTA/nav)
         \
          \   (diagonal scan)
           \
3. Bottom-left (supporting info) -> 4. Bottom-right (final CTA)
```
Best for: landing pages, app home screens, onboarding screens, ads.
Rule: Place your PRIMARY action at position 2 or 4.

**F-Pattern** (for content-heavy pages)
```
1. Top horizontal scan ========================
2. Second horizontal scan ============
3. Vertical scan down left side
   |
   |
   |
```
Best for: blog posts, news, settings screens, lists.
Rule: Front-load the first two words of every line/heading.

### Visual Hierarchy Tools (ranked by power)
1. **Size** -- bigger = more important. Period.
2. **Color/Contrast** -- bright on muted background wins attention
3. **Position** -- top-left gets seen first (Western reading)
4. **Whitespace** -- isolation = importance. Give key elements breathing room.
5. **Typography weight** -- bold vs. regular creates instant hierarchy
6. **Imagery** -- humans look where faces look (eye-tracking proven)
7. **Motion** -- animation draws the eye but use sparingly

### Color Psychology

| Color | Association | Use When |
|---|---|---|
| **Blue** | Trust, calm, stability, professionalism | Finance, health, tech, corporate |
| **Green** | Growth, health, nature, money | Wellness, sustainability, finance |
| **Purple** | Creativity, luxury, wisdom | Premium products, creative tools |
| **Red** | Energy, urgency, passion, danger | CTAs, alerts, food, entertainment |
| **Orange** | Warmth, enthusiasm, friendliness | Casual brands, calls-to-action |
| **Yellow** | Optimism, clarity, warmth | Accents, warnings, cheerful brands |
| **Black** | Sophistication, luxury, power | Luxury, fashion, minimalism |
| **White** | Purity, simplicity, space | Health, tech, minimalist brands |
| **Teal/Cyan** | Balance, clarity, refreshment | Wellness, modern tech |

Color increases brand recognition by up to 80% (research-backed).

### The 60-30-10 Color Rule
- **60%** -- Dominant color. Background, large surfaces. Sets the mood.
- **30%** -- Secondary color. Supporting elements, cards, sections. Creates contrast.
- **10%** -- Accent color. CTAs, highlights, badges. Draws the eye.

Example: McDonald's = 60% red, 30% yellow, 10% white.

### Typography Psychology

| Type | Communicates | Examples | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Serif** | Trust, tradition, authority | Times, Garamond, Merriweather | Established brands, editorial |
| **Sans-serif** | Modern, clean, approachable | Helvetica, Inter, SF Pro | Tech, apps, startups |
| **Rounded sans** | Warmth, friendliness, safety | Nunito, Varela Round, Quicksand | Wellness, children, casual |
| **Geometric sans** | Precision, contemporary | Futura, Montserrat, Poppins | Design-forward, modern |
| **Monospace** | Technical, code, precision | JetBrains Mono, Fira Code | Dev tools, data |
| **Handwritten/Script** | Personal, creative, human | Caveat, Dancing Script | Invitations, personal brands |

Key stat: Serif fonts increase perceived trustworthiness by 40%. Rounded fonts increase perceived warmth.

**Weight hierarchy in practice:**
- **Bold/Black** -- headlines, key numbers, primary actions
- **Semibold/Medium** -- subheadings, labels, secondary emphasis
- **Regular** -- body text, descriptions
- **Light** -- captions, metadata, tertiary info

## D. DESIGN PRINCIPLES THAT SELL

### Simplicity (Less = More Conversion)
- Every additional element on screen reduces conversion by forcing choice
- Hick's Law: time to decide increases logarithmically with number of options
- Rule: One screen, one purpose, one primary action
- Apple, Google, Stripe all maximize whitespace

### Alignment and Grid Systems
- 8pt grid: all spacing and sizing in multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48...)
- Consistent alignment creates unconscious trust
- Misalignment, even by 1-2px, creates subconscious unease
- iOS uses the 8pt grid natively in SF design system

### Gestalt Principles Applied to Product Design

| Principle | Rule | Application |
|---|---|---|
| **Proximity** | Things close together are perceived as related | Group related controls. Separate unrelated sections with whitespace. |
| **Similarity** | Things that look alike are perceived as related | Use consistent card styles for same-type content. Different styles = different function. |
| **Closure** | Brain completes incomplete shapes | Progress rings, partial circles, logo design (Apple logo, WWF panda) |
| **Continuity** | Eye follows smooth paths | Horizontal scroll, timeline views, onboarding flows |
| **Figure/Ground** | Brain separates foreground from background | Modal sheets, overlays, blurred backgrounds behind content |
| **Common fate** | Things moving together are perceived as grouped | Animations where related elements move in sync |

### Don Norman's 3 Levels of Emotional Design

**Level 1: VISCERAL (first impression, automatic)**
- What it looks like before you use it
- Color, shape, texture, feel
- "Do I want to pick this up / tap this?"
- Design action: Beautiful screenshots, polished icon, smooth animations
- For MARIS: The app icon, the first screen, the color palette

**Level 2: BEHAVIORAL (during use, subconscious)**
- Does it work? Is it easy? Does it feel good to use?
- Responsiveness, flow, predictability
- "Did that do what I expected?"
- Design action: Fast transitions, clear feedback, intuitive navigation
- For MARIS: Chat feels instant, dashboard loads fast, plans are easy to create

**Level 3: REFLECTIVE (after use, conscious)**
- What does using this say about me?
- Status, identity, meaning, memory
- "I'm the kind of person who uses this"
- Design action: Brand story, social proof, personal growth narrative
- For MARIS: "I use this to protect my focus" -- identity statement, not utility

The reflective level is where brand loyalty lives. Branding is a reflective-level exercise.

MARIS falls into **Category 4: Real Word (Suggestive)** -- it's a real Latin word that doesn't describe the product but has evocative qualities.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |

- **Subtitle/tagline** that explains immediately: "MARIS -- your focus shield" or "MARIS -- protect your mental space"
- **Visual identity** that reinforces calm-focus, not clinical-therapy
- **App Store subtitle**: "Focus & Mental Clarity" or "Protect Your Day"

## F. VISUAL IDENTITY DIRECTION FOR MARIS

### Brand Brief (filled in)
| Component | MARIS Definition |
|---|---|
| **Mission** | Protect people's mental space so they can focus on what matters |
| **Vision** | The default tool people open to check in with themselves |
| **Values** | Privacy-first. Never prescriptive. Concrete over abstract. |
| **Personality** | Calm friend who's smarter than they let on. Not a therapist. Not a coach. |
| **Voice** | Direct, warm, short sentences. "Eso pesa. Cuanto hace?" Not "I sense some tension." |
| **Audience** | 22-35, overwhelmed professionals/students who won't download a "therapy app" |
| **Positioning** | The only focus tool that actually understands how you feel today |
| **Promise** | We read the room so you don't have to explain yourself |

### Color System (60-30-10)

**Direction: "Deep ocean calm" -- not pastel wellness, not clinical blue**

| Role | Color | Hex (suggested) | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **60% Dominant** | Deep navy / off-black | `#0D1B2A` or `#1B2838` | Authority without coldness. "Night ocean." Grounds everything. |
| **30% Secondary** | Warm slate / muted teal | `#415A77` or `#3A6B7E` | Depth, intelligence, calm. Not baby-blue wellness. |
| **10% Accent** | Soft gold / warm amber | `#D4A574` or `#E8B86D` | Warmth, insight, "moment of clarity." The star that appears for takeaways. |

Why NOT pastel blue/green: Every wellness app uses them. MARIS needs to feel like a TOOL, not a meditation cushion. Dark backgrounds = focus mode (like code editors, dark mode, cinema). The target audience (22-35 overwhelmed professionals) already prefers dark mode.

**Light mode alternative:** Swap dominant to warm off-white `#F5F0EB` (paper, not screen-white), keep slate and gold. Same hierarchy.

### Typography System

| Role | Font | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Display / Headlines** | SF Pro Display or Inter | Semibold | Clean, geometric, modern. iOS-native feel. |
| **Body / Chat** | SF Pro Text or Inter | Regular | Maximum readability at small sizes |
| **Accent / Labels** | SF Pro Rounded | Medium | Warmth without losing authority. Use for moods, tags, pills. |
| **Numbers / Data** | SF Mono or JetBrains Mono | Regular | Physics engine data, stats -- technical precision feel |

Why rounded for accents only: Full rounded font = "children's app." Rounded ONLY on soft elements (mood dots, health pills, tags) creates contrast: the app is serious, but it sees you as human.

### Imagery & Iconography Style
- **Icons**: Outlined, 1.5px stroke, rounded caps. NOT filled. Airiness > solidity.
- **Illustrations**: None. Zero. Let the data and whitespace speak.
- **Imagery**: Abstract gradients only -- deep ocean tones, never photos of people meditating.
- **Motion**: Slow, deliberate. Ease-in-out curves. Nothing bouncy. Think: breathing rhythm.
- **Haptics**: Light impact for confirmations. Medium for completions. Never aggressive.

### Design System: "Calm Focus" Not "Clinical Therapy"

**What to AVOID:**
- Pastel gradients (Calm, Headspace territory)
- Heart icons, brain icons, smiley faces (therapy app signals)
- The word "wellness" anywhere
- Purple + pink combinations (too spiritual)
- Bubble/cloud shapes (too soft, childish)
- Stock photos of people journaling on mountains

**What to EMBRACE:**
- Dark spaces with purposeful light (cinema, focus mode)
- Cards with generous padding (breathing room = Gestalt proximity principle)
- Single-line labels (not paragraphs)
- The gold accent sparingly -- when it appears, it MEANS something (takeaway, insight, completion)
- Horizontal rules and subtle separators (structure = trust)
- Status through shape, not emoji (stability ring, capacity bar)

### Norman's 3 Levels Applied to MARIS

**Visceral (first 3 seconds):**
- App icon: Simple "M" lettermark in gold on dark navy. Or: abstract wave/shield shape.
- Splash: Dark background, "MARIS" in light type, subtle gold underscore animation
- First impression: "This looks like a premium tool, not a free therapy chatbot"

**Behavioral (daily use):**
- Dashboard loads in <1s with today's data
- Chat responds with streaming text (no loading spinner sitting)
- Plans can be created in 1 tap (voice) or 3 taps (manual)
- Night review is 2 taps for good days, 4 taps for heavy days
- Physics engine data is visual, never numerical soup

**Reflective (identity):**
- "I use MARIS to protect my focus" -- says something about the user
- Not "I use a therapy app" -- stigma-free positioning
- Weekly digest makes users feel SEEN, not analyzed
- Sharing a takeaway feels like sharing wisdom, not vulnerability

### App Store Presentation

**Title**: MARIS -- Focus Shield
**Subtitle**: Protect your day. Understand your patterns.
**Category**: Productivity (NOT Health & Fitness, NOT Medical)
**Screenshots** (in order, Z-pattern logic):

2. Chat in action -- "Check in. No judgment."
3. Physics engine visualization -- "See your patterns"
4. Night review -- "Close the day in 30 seconds"

**What the listing communicates**: This is a focus and productivity tool that happens to understand emotions. Not a therapy app that happens to have a to-do list.

## G. QUICK REFERENCE: FRAMEWORKS CHEAT SHEET

### Branding Order of Operations
1. Strategy (who, why, what position)
2. Name (does it fit the strategy?)
3. Visual identity (does it FEEL like the strategy?)
4. Design system (can I apply it consistently?)
5. Content/marketing (does every touchpoint match?)

### Name Evaluation Checklist
- [ ] Under 4 syllables
- [ ] 5-12 letters
- [ ] Pronounceable after hearing once
- [ ] Spellable after hearing once
- [ ] No competitor conflicts
- [ ] No negative meanings in target markets
- [ ] Domain or variant available
- [ ] App Store name available
- [ ] Supports a bigger idea (not just a feature)

### Visual Hierarchy Checklist
- [ ] One primary action per screen
- [ ] Z-pattern for minimal layouts
- [ ] F-pattern for content-heavy screens
- [ ] 60-30-10 color distribution
- [ ] 8pt grid spacing
- [ ] Bold for primary, medium for secondary, regular for body
- [ ] Whitespace used to create grouping (Gestalt proximity)
- [ ] Accent color reserved for meaningful moments

### Emotional Design Checklist
- [ ] Visceral: Does it look premium in the first 3 seconds?
- [ ] Behavioral: Does every action feel instant and predictable?
- [ ] Reflective: Does using this make the user feel good about themselves?
